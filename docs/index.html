Revenue Distribution

Most purchases are moderate, but premium items pull revenue upward

Main conclusions

  • The average purchase price is 304.35 while the median is 174.02, which shows a strong right tail.
  • Premium items matter disproportionately: p90 is 795.90 and p99 is 1628.07.
  • This portfolio is not driven by a uniform basket of low-value orders; a smaller set of expensive products has outsized impact.
Use caution: there is no quantity field, so this is purchase-price distribution, not complete order revenue.

Trend

November growth came from more buyers and more purchase events, not a higher ticket

Main conclusions

  • Revenue proxy increased from 230.0M in October to 275.2M in November.
  • Purchasing users grew from 347k to 442k, so growth was broad-based rather than concentrated in a few buyers.
  • Average purchase price fell from 309.56 to 300.12, which suggests the uplift came from volume more than pricing power.
With only October and November 2019, this should be read as a short trading pulse, not a full seasonal pattern.

Customers

A small group of very heavy buyers likely contributes outsized value

Main conclusions

  • The top observed customer generated 344,153.29 in revenue proxy across 590 purchase events.
  • Several leading customers show very high average purchase prices, hinting at reseller, business, or premium-buying behavior.
  • This is a strong signal that RFM and VIP segmentation will be valuable in the next phase.
This is only a two-month window, so some customers may look low-frequency simply because earlier history is missing.

Products

Top-selling products are dominated by premium smartphones

Main conclusions

  • The top product is Apple smartphone 1005115 with 33.0M in revenue proxy.
  • The top list is heavily concentrated in Apple and Samsung smartphones, showing that product mix is highly skewed.
  • Revenue leadership here reflects both strong demand and high ticket prices, so conversion and price mix must be read together.
Product rankings can overstate portfolio health if a few hero SKUs mask weak depth across the rest of the assortment.

Basket

Most purchase sessions are single-item, so cross-sell looks underdeveloped

Main conclusions

  • Average items per purchase session is 1.18 and the median is 1.0.
  • The purchase journey is dominated by single-item buying rather than broad baskets.
  • This points to either a considered-purchase catalog or missed bundling and cross-sell opportunities.
There is no order ID, so basket size is approximated at the purchase-session level rather than true cart checkout level.

Category Funnel

Some categories combine very high traffic with very high purchase intent

Main conclusions

  • Among meaningful categories, kids.fmcg.diapers and electronics.smartphone are standout converters.
  • Smartphones are especially important because they pair strong conversion with very large traffic volume.
  • High-traffic, low-conversion categories are where merchandising and UX work can move the business most.
This funnel is built at the user_session + product_id level to reduce repeated-view noise.

Category Mix

Smartphones are the portfolio engine by a very large margin

Main conclusions

  • electronics.smartphone alone contributes 66.29% of category revenue proxy.
  • The next categories are much smaller: TVs at 4.13% and notebooks at 3.89%.
  • This is a highly concentrated portfolio, which can be efficient but increases assortment and dependency risk.
The Unknown category still holds 10.45% of revenue proxy, so metadata quality is materially affecting category reporting.

Brand Mix

Apple and Samsung dominate brand-level value creation

Main conclusions

  • Apple contributes 47.26% of brand revenue proxy and Samsung adds 20.05%.
  • Together they account for 67.31% of brand value, which is a major concentration signal.
  • Brand power, supplier leverage, and pricing strategy likely matter as much as category performance in this business.
A brand-led portfolio can be strong commercially, but it also increases exposure to vendor concentration and pricing pressure.

Opportunity Matrix

The clearest optimization targets are high-traffic categories that convert poorly

Main conclusions

  • The core portfolio sits in High Traffic / High Conversion, generating about 434.3M in revenue proxy.
  • The main “fix” categories include computers.desktop, furniture.living_room.sofa, and apparel.shoes.
  • The main “scale” categories include appliances.environment.air_conditioner and electronics.camera.photo, which show decent conversion but limited traffic.
This view is excellent for prioritization: defend core winners, fix wasted traffic, and scale underexposed high-intent categories.

RFM Segmentation

Customer value is concentrated, and retention priorities are already visible

Main conclusions

  • Champions contribute 42.39% of revenue proxy and are the core value base.
  • Cannot Lose Them contribute another 16.03%, which means a meaningful share of value is already sitting in weakening recency.
  • Potential Loyalists are the most attractive upgrade audience for CRM and repeat-purchase programs.
RFM uses purchase sessions as the frequency proxy because the dataset has no order ID.

Statistical Analysis

The main commercial patterns are statistically real, not just visible in charts

Main conclusions

  • Smartphone conversion is 3.97% versus 1.71% for the rest of the catalog, a 132.59% uplift.
  • November median purchase price is 5.49% lower than October, so the growth month had a cheaper price mix.
  • Apple median purchase price is 231.94% higher than Samsung, confirming a much more premium pricing position.
With a dataset this large, p-values become tiny very easily. Business meaning should be judged from uplift, concentration, and median differences.

Retention

Repeat customers are not the majority, but they drive most of the value

Main conclusions

  • Only 36.84% of customers are repeat customers, but they generate 73.77% of revenue proxy.
  • The October cohort shows 26.3% month-1 retention, which means most first-time buyers still do not come back next month.
  • The median time to second purchase is only 3 days, so the first week after purchase is a critical CRM window.
This is still a short-window retention view because the dataset only covers two months.

Decision Layer

The analysis now points to a prioritized action plan, not just descriptive findings

Main conclusions

  • The top priorities are protecting smartphone economics, fixing high-traffic low-conversion categories, and launching retention plays for high-value dormant customers.
  • This moves the project into senior-level decision support because the analysis now explicitly ranks what should happen first.
  • The portfolio does not need more traffic first; it needs better monetization and better retention of already valuable behavior.
Prioritization is where analytics becomes strategy: the goal is not to explain everything, but to sequence the next best moves.

Experiment Roadmap

The next phase should be test-driven, not intuition-driven

Main conclusions

  • Best near-term A/B test: redesign product detail pages for high-traffic low-conversion categories like desktops.
  • Best lifecycle test: repeat-purchase flow for Potential Loyalists and win-back flow for Cannot Lose Them.
  • Best merchandising test: bundle recommendations on single-item flows and traffic reallocation to niche high-conversion categories.
Strong analytics should end in measurable experiments with a primary metric and guardrails, not only in recommendations.

Data Gap

Country analysis is not available from this dataset

The uploaded data has no country or geography field, so a top-countries chart would be invented and analytically invalid. Any geographic expansion or localization decisions will need a separate source with shipping, billing, or regional metadata.

Executive Takeaways

What the business should do next

  • Protect smartphones as the commercial engine, but reduce concentration risk over time.
  • Prioritize high-traffic, low-conversion categories for near-term conversion improvement.
  • Invest in bundling and cross-sell because most purchase sessions remain single-item.
  • Use RFM and cohort timing to build distinct retention plays for Champions, Potential Loyalists, and Cannot Lose Them.
  • Run experiments in sequence: fix wasted traffic first, then improve retention, then diversify efficient growth pockets.
  • Bring in geography and order-level data if the next phase needs market expansion or true basket economics.
